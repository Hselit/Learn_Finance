Financial System

The financial system is like a network that helps money and resources move around in an economy. It includes banks, markets, rules, and mechanisms that make it easier for people and businesses to save, borrow, invest, and exchange money. It also includes organizations that keep an eye on the system to make sure things are fair and stable. Financial institutions, like banks and insurance companies, are part of the system. They help people save money, give out loans, and provide other financial services. Financial markets, such as the stock market, bond market, and currency market, are places where people buy and sell things like company shares, bonds, and foreign currencies. Rules and regulations are important to keep the system fair and safe. There are organizations that make and enforce these rules to protect people and make sure everyone follows the same standards. Payment systems are also part of the financial system. They help people transfer money between each other, like when you pay for something using a credit card or make an online payment. Overall, the financial system helps people manage their money, invest in businesses, and make the economy work. It's like a big network that connects everyone and everything related to money in an organized way.

Types of Financial System

  • Financial Institutions

  • Financial Markets

  • Financial Instruments

  • Financial Services

Explaination

  • Financial Institutions:

    Financial institutions include banks, credit unions, insurance companies, investment firms, and other entities that offer financial services. They play a crucial role in mobilizing funds from savers and channeling them towards borrowers and investors. Financial institutions provide services such as accepting deposits, providing loans, facilitating investments, and managing risks.

  • Financial Markets:

    Financial markets provide a platform for buying and selling financial instruments such as stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies, and derivatives. They facilitate the transfer of funds from investors to borrowers or sellers and enable price discovery and liquidity. Examples of financial markets include stock exchanges, bond markets, commodity markets, and foreign exchange markets.

  • Regulatory Bodies:

    Regulatory bodies are government or independent agencies responsible for overseeing and regulating the financial system. They establish and enforce rules, regulations, and standards to ensure fair and transparent practices, protect investors, maintain stability, and mitigate systemic risks. Regulatory bodies can include central banks, securities and exchange commissions, banking authorities, and insurance regulatory agencies.

  • Financial Instruments:

    Financial instruments are contracts or agreements that represent a financial claim or ownership. They include stocks, bonds, options, futures, mortgages, loans, and insurance policies. These instruments allow individuals, businesses, and institutions to raise capital, manage risks, invest, and save.

  • Central Banks:

    Central banks are the primary monetary authorities in a country or a group of countries. They are responsible for formulating and implementing monetary policies, regulating banks, managing the money supply, and maintaining financial stability. Central banks also act as lenders of last resort to provide liquidity to financial institutions during times of crisis.

  • Financial Services:

    The range of services provided by financial institutions and professionals to individuals, businesses, and organizations. These services involve the management, investment, and allocation of financial resources, as well as the facilitation of financial transactions.

Financial Markets

Financial markets refer to platforms where buyers and sellers engage in trading various financial assets, such as stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities, and derivatives. These markets facilitate the allocation of capital and enable investors to buy or sell financial instruments based on their investment objectives and risk tolerance.

Functions of Financial Markets

  • Price Discovery: Financial markets determine the prices of financial assets based on supply and demand dynamics.

  • Liquidity: They provide liquidity, enabling investors to easily buy or sell assets without significant price impact.

  • Capital Allocation: Financial markets facilitate the allocation of capital by channeling funds from savers to borrowers or companies in need of financing.

  • Risk Management: They offer risk management tools, such as derivatives, allowing investors to hedge against market fluctuations.

  • Investor Protection: Financial markets operate under regulatory frameworks that aim to protect investors' interests and ensure fair trading practices.

Market Participants

  • Individual Investors

  • Institutional Investors (banks, pension funds, insurance companies)

  • Trader

  • Brokers and Dealers

  • Exchanges

  • Regulatory Authorities

Importance of Financial Markets

Financial markets play a vital role in the economy by:

  • Facilitating capital formation and economic growth

  • Providing opportunities for investments and wealth creation

  • Enabling efficient allocation of resources

  • Supporting risk management and hedging strategies

  • Contributing to price stability and economic stability

Understanding financial markets is crucial for investors and participants to make informed decisions and navigate the complexities of the global financial system.

Types of Financial Market

  • By the Nature of the Claim:

    • Equity Market:

      Involves buying and selling shares of publicly traded companies. Example: Buying shares of Tesla Inc. on the NASDAQ stock exchange with the expectation of capital appreciation.

    • Debt Market:

      Involves buying and selling of debt securities issued by governments and corporations. Example: Investing in US Treasury bonds to earn fixed interest income or purchasing corporate bonds issued by IBM Corporation.

    • By the Maturity on Claim:

    • Short Tenure (less than 1 year)

      Money Market:

      Deals with short-term borrowing and lending of funds, typically for periods of one year or less. It includes instruments such as Treasury bills, certificates of deposit, commercial paper, and repurchase agreements. Example: Investing in a 3-month Treasury bill issued by the government.

    • Longer Maturity (more than 1 year)

      • Capital Market:

        Deals with long-term borrowing and lending of funds, typically for periods exceeding one year.

      • Equity Market:

        Involves the buying and selling of shares of publicly traded companies. Example: Purchasing shares of Apple Inc. on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE)

        .
      • Bond Market:

        Involves the buying and selling of debt securities issued by governments and corporations. Example: Investing in corporate bonds issued by Microsoft Corporation.

      • Real Estate Market:

        Involves the buying, selling, and renting of properties such as residential and commercial buildings. Example: Buying a residential property for investment purposes or renting an office space for business operations.

  • Based on the Organisational Structure:

    • Exchange-Traded Market:

      A marketplace where people can buy and sell financial products like stocks, bonds, commodities, and derivatives. It's a centralized location with regulated and organized trading. Prices are public, and fair trading practices are ensured. Examples include stock exchanges and commodity exchanges.

    • Over the Counter Market (OTC):

      A decentralized marketplace where trading happens directly between two parties without a central exchange. Buyers and sellers negotiate and trade through phone, email, or electronic platforms.

  • Based on the timing of delivery:

    • Cash Market (Spot Market):

      Financial instruments are bought and sold for immediate delivery and payment. Transactions are settled "on the spot," meaning the buyer pays and the seller delivers the asset immediately. This market deals with the immediate exchange of goods or securities for cash at the current market price.

      Example: Cash market transactions occur when you buy fruits, vegetables, or any other product at a grocery store by paying cash.

    • Futures Market:

      Involves trading standardized contracts called futures contracts. These contracts specify the delivery of an underlying asset at a predetermined future date and price. Parties involved agree to buy or sell the asset on a specified future date, regardless of the current market price at that time. Futures contracts are traded on exchanges with standardized terms and conditions (including quantity, quality, and delivery date).

      Example: A farmer entering a futures contract to sell a certain quantity of corn to a buyer at a predetermined price and delivery date. The transaction will be settled at the agreed-upon future date, regardless of the current market price of corn.

    • Foreign Exchange Market

      The Foreign Exchange Market facilitates the buying and selling of different currencies. It allows individuals, businesses, and financial institutions to exchange one currency for another, enabling international trade and investment. Imagine exchanging US Dollars (USD) for Euros (EUR) before a trip to Europe.

    • Commodity Market

      The Commodity Market deals with buying and selling of raw materials like gold, oil, agricultural products, and industrial metals. Here, you might purchase gold bullion as a hedge against inflation or trade contracts for crude oil futures.

    • Cryptocurrency Market

      The Cryptocurrency Market involves buying and selling digital currencies like Bitcoin, Ethereum, and others. These cryptocurrencies operate on decentralized networks using blockchain technology, allowing secure and direct transactions between individuals (peer-to-peer). An example is buying Bitcoin (BTC) or Ethereum (ETH).

Capital Market

The capital market is a financial market where long-term securities such as stocks and bonds are bought and sold. It facilitates the transfer of capital from investors to businesses or governments in need of funding for various purposes.

Types of Capital Market

  • Primary Market

    The primary market is where new securities are issued and sold for the first time. It allows companies and governments to raise fresh capital by selling stocks or bonds directly to investors.

  • Secondary Market

    The secondary market is where previously issued securities are traded among investors. It provides liquidity to investors by allowing them to buy or sell existing securities without involving the issuing companies.

Functions of Capital Market

  • Facilitating Capital Formation: The capital market enables businesses and governments to raise funds for investment and expansion by issuing securities to interested investors.

  • Providing Liquidity: The secondary market allows investors to buy and sell securities, providing liquidity and ensuring the marketability of investments.

  • Price Determination: Capital markets facilitate the discovery of fair prices for securities through the forces of supply and demand.

  • Risk Transfer: Investors can transfer their investment risks by buying and selling securities in the capital market.

  • Investor Protection: Capital markets often have regulations and mechanisms in place to protect the interests of investors and maintain market integrity.

Primary Market

The primary market, also known as the new issue market, is where securities are issued and sold for the first time by companies or governments to raise capital. It is the initial offering of securities to the public.

Functions of the Primary Market

  • Raising Capital: The primary market enables companies to raise funds for business expansion, project financing, debt repayment, research and development, and other capital requirements.

  • Facilitating Ownership Transfer: Through the primary market, ownership of a company is transferred from private ownership to public ownership by issuing shares to the general public.

  • Price Discovery: The primary market helps in determining the initial price of securities based on market demand and investor appetite, establishing the fair value of the securities.

  • Increasing Market Liquidity: By listing on the primary market, companies provide an opportunity for investors to buy and sell their shares, increasing market liquidity.

  • Underwriting: Involves an agreement where an underwriter, typically a financial institution, guarantees the sale of a company's shares by purchasing the unsold shares or finding buyers for them.

Types of Primary Market Offerings

  • Initial Public Offering (IPO): An IPO is the first sale of securities by a company to the public. It allows companies to raise capital and become publicly traded entities.

  • Follow-on Public Offering (FPO): An FPO is a subsequent offering of securities by a company that is already listed on the stock exchange. It helps companies raise additional capital for expansion or other purposes.

  • Rights Issue: A rights issue allows existing shareholders to purchase additional shares of the company at a discounted price in proportion to their existing holdings.

  • Private Placement: Private placement involves the sale of securities directly to institutional investors or a select group of investors, bypassing the public offering process.

  • Qualified Institutional Placement (QIP): A QIP is a private placement of securities exclusively to qualified institutional buyers, such as banks, financial institutions, and mutual funds.

Secondary Market

The secondary market, also known as the stock market or stock exchange, is where already issued securities are bought and sold among investors. It provides a platform for trading previously issued securities.

Examples of Secondary Market

  • Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE): It is one of the oldest stock exchanges in Asia and the largest stock exchange in India.

  • National Stock Exchange (NSE): It is the leading stock exchange in India that provides a modern, fully automated trading platform.

Functions of the Secondary Market

  • Liquidity: The secondary market provides liquidity to investors, allowing them to buy or sell securities at any time, based on supply and demand.

  • Determination: It helps in determining the fair market price of securities based on the forces of supply and demand.

  • Facilitating Trading: The secondary market facilitates the buying and selling of securities through various trading mechanisms, such as stock exchanges and electronic trading platforms.

  • Investment Opportunities: It provides opportunities for investors to diversify their portfolios and invest in a wide range of securities, including stocks, bonds, derivatives, and exchange-traded funds.

Types of Secondary Market

  • Stock Market

    It is the market where shares of publicly listed companies are bought and sold.

  • Bond Market

    It is the market for buying and selling debt securities, such as government bonds, corporate bonds, and municipal bonds.

  • Derivatives Market

    It is the market for trading financial contracts derived from underlying assets, such as futures contracts, options contracts, and swaps.

  • Commodity Market

    It is the market for trading commodities, such as gold, silver, crude oil, agricultural products, etc.

  • Foreign Exchange Market

    It is the market where currencies are bought and sold, facilitating international trade and investment.

SEBI - Securities and Exchange Board of India

About SEBI

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) is the regulatory authority for the securities market in India. It was established in 1988 and operates under the Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992.

Uses and Importance

  • Regulation and Oversight

    SEBI regulates and oversees various entities in the securities market, ensuring fair practices, transparency, and investor protection.

  • Protection

    SEBI safeguards the interests of investors by promoting disclosure of accurate information, preventing fraudulent practices, and enhancing investor awareness.

  • Market Development

    SEBI fosters the development and growth of the securities market, introducing reforms to enhance efficiency, liquidity, and integrity.

  • Regulatory Framework

    SEBI establishes and enforces rules and regulations governing aspects such as listing, trading, corporate governance, and disclosure requirements.

  • Market Surveillance and Enforcement

    SEBI monitors the securities market, detects manipulative activities, and takes enforcement actions against violators.

SEBI's Role

SEBI plays a crucial role in maintaining market integrity, investor confidence, and the overall stability of the securities market in India. It regulates market participants, enforces compliance, and ensures a fair and transparent environment for investors.

Investors and Traders

Investors

Definition of Investors

Investors are individuals or institutions who allocate capital with the expectation of generating a return or gaining ownership in an asset. They aim for long-term growth and income by investing in various financial instruments.

Types of Investors

  • Individual Investors: Retail investors who invest their personal savings in the stock market, mutual funds, fixed deposits, etc.

  • Institutional Investors: Entities such as banks, insurance companies, pension funds, and hedge funds that invest on behalf of their clients or policyholders.

    • Domestic Institutional Investors (DIIs): Institutional investors based in India. They play a crucial role in the financial market. (Eg. Life Insurance Corporation of India (LIC), State Bank of India (SBI) Mutual Fund)

    • Foreign Institutional Investors (FIIs): Foreign entities that invest in the Indian financial markets.

  • Angel Investors: High-net-worth individuals who provide capital to startups and early-stage companies in exchange for equity ownership.

  • Venture Capitalists (VCs): Investment firms that provide funding to startups in exchange for equity stakes and actively participate in the company's growth.

Role of Investors

  • Capital Allocation: Allocate capital to different assets and businesses, providing them with funds for growth and expansion.

  • Risk Management: Diversify investments across various asset classes to manage risk and minimize potential losses.

  • Long-Term Growth: Aim for long-term capital appreciation and wealth creation through investment strategies.

  • Corporate Governance: Institutional investors play a significant role by participating in shareholder meetings and exercising

    voting rights.

  • Market Stability: Contribute to market stability by providing liquidity and holding investments for longer durations.

Traders

Definition of Traders

Traders are individuals or entities who engage in buying and selling financial instruments in the market with the primary goal of making short-term profits. They capitalize on market fluctuations and employ various trading strategies.

Types of Traders

  • Day Traders: Execute trades within a single trading day and close all positions before the market closes.

  • Swing Traders: Hold positions for a few days to several weeks, aiming to capture short-term price movements.

  • Position Traders: Hold positions for an extended period, ranging from weeks to months or even years, based on long-term trends and fundamental analysis.

  • Algorithmic Traders: Use computer algorithms to execute trades automatically, leveraging quantitative models and high-speed trading systems.

  • Arbitrageurs: Take advantage of price discrepancies in different markets or securities to make risk-free profits.

Role of Traders

  • Price Discovery: Contribute to price discovery by actively buying and selling securities, leading to efficient pricing in the market.

  • Liquidity Provision: Provide liquidity to the market by executing trades and facilitating smooth transactions.

  • Market Efficiency: Help in improving market efficiency by quickly reacting to news, information, and market trends.

  • Speculation: Engage in speculative trading to profit from short-term price movements, leveraging their skills and market analysis.

  • Volatility Management: Use various strategies to manage and capitalize on market volatility, reducing potential risks.

Share Transfer Process

Share transfer refers to the process of transferring ownership of shares from one party (transferor) to another party (transferee). It involves legal and financial procedures to facilitate this change in ownership.

Understanding the Process with an Example

Imagine Mr. A wants to sell 100 shares of XYZ Company to Mr. B. Here's how the share transfer process unfolds:

  1. Execution of Transfer Deed:

    The first step involves a transfer deed. This document, signed by both Mr. A and Mr. B, details the transfer agreement. It includes information like their names (transferor and transferee), the number of shares being transferred, and the agreed-upon consideration amount for the transfer.

  2. Clearance by the Company:

    Next, Mr. A submits the transfer deed to XYZ Company. The company verifies the transfer details and ensures compliance with their rules and regulations. Upon successful verification, the company approves the transfer.

  3. Clearance by the Depository (if applicable):

    If the shares are held electronically (demat), Mr. A's depository participant (DP) or central depository verifies the transfer details. The DP then initiates the transfer process within the electronic system.

  4. Transfer in Depository System (if applicable):

    (For demat shares) The shares are electronically transferred from Mr. A's demat account to Mr. B's demat account within the depository system. This transfer is reflected in the electronic records maintained by the depository.

  5. Update of Shareholding:

    Once the transfer is complete, Mr. B's ownership of the 100 shares is recorded in both the company's share register and his demat account. Mr. A's ownership of the transferred shares is extinguished.

    6. Depositories in India

    India has two major depositories facilitating the electronic holding and transfer of shares:

    National Securities Depository Limited (NSDL):

    NSDL is a leading depository offering dematerialization and settlement services for various securities, including equities and debt instruments.

    Functions of NSDL include:

    • Opening and maintaining demat accounts for investors.

    • Electronic settlement of trades in stock exchanges.

    • Safekeeping and maintaining securities in electronic form.

    • Transfer of securities through a book-entry system.

    • Facilitating corporate actions like dividends, bonus issues, etc.

    Central Depository Services Limited (CDSL):

    CDSL is another prominent depository offering dematerialization, settlement, and other related services for securities.

    Functions of CDSL include:

    • Settlement of trades in stock exchanges.

    • Maintaining electronic records of securities.

    • Facilitating transfer of securities electronically.

    • Handling corporate actions like dividends, rights issues, etc.

    • Providing demat account services to investors.

    By eliminating the need for physical share certificates and enabling seamless electronic transfers, these depositories play a crucial role in promoting efficiency, transparency, and security in share transfer processes.

Ratios

  1. Return on Assets (ROA)

    Definition: ROA measures how efficiently a company utilizes its assets to generate profits. It indicates the percentage of each rupee of assets that generates net income.

    Formula: ROA = (Net Income / Average Total Assets) x 100

    Uses: ROA helps assess the profitability and efficiency of asset utilization. Higher ROA indicates better performance. For example, a company with an ROA of 15% means it generates ₹0.15 of net income for every rupee of assets invested.

  2. Return on Investment (ROI)

    Definition: ROI evaluates the return on an investment relative to its cost. It shows the percentage gain or loss on an investment.

    Formula: ROI = (Net Profit / Cost of Investment) x 100

    Uses: ROI helps assess the profitability of an investment. It enables comparison between different investment options. For example, if an investment of ₹10,000 generates a net profit of ₹2,000, the ROI would be 20%, indicating a 20% return on the investment.

  3. Gross Margin Ratio

    Definition: The gross margin ratio indicates the profitability of a company's core operations by measuring the percentage of revenue that remains after deducting the cost of goods sold.

    Formula: Gross Margin Ratio = (Gross Profit / Revenue) x 100

    Uses: The gross margin ratio helps assess the profitability of a company's products or services. Higher gross margin ratio implies better profitability. For example, if a company has a gross margin ratio of 40%, it means it retains ₹0.40 as gross profit for every rupee of revenue generated.

  4. Operating Margin Ratio

    Definition: The operating margin ratio assesses a company's operating efficiency and profitability by measuring the percentage of each rupee of revenue that translates into operating income.

    Formula: Operating Margin Ratio = (Operating Income / Revenue) x 100

    Uses: The operating margin ratio helps evaluate the profitability of a company's core operations. Higher operating margin ratio indicates better operating efficiency. For example, if a company has an operating margin ratio of 15%, it means it generates ₹0.15 of operating income for every rupee of revenue earned.

  5. Debt-to-Equity Ratio

    Definition: The debt-to-equity ratio evaluates a company's capital structure and financial leverage by comparing its total debt to shareholders' equity.

    Formula: Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity

    Uses: The debt-to-equity ratio helps assess a company's financial risk and solvency. It indicates the proportion of financing provided by debt and equity. A lower ratio is generally preferred, as it signifies a lower risk of financial distress. For example, if a company has a debt-to-equity ratio of 0.8, it means it has ₹0.80 of debt for every rupee of shareholders' equity.

  6. Current Ratio

    Definition: The current ratio measures a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations using its current assets.

    Formula: Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

    Use: The current ratio helps assess a company's short-term liquidity. A ratio of 2 or higher is generally considered favorable, indicating a strong ability to cover short-term obligations. For example, if a company has current assets of ₹1,00,000 and current liabilities of ₹50,000, the current ratio would be 2, indicating a healthy liquidity position.

  7. Earnings Per Share (EPS)

    Definition: EPS measures the portion of a company's profit allocated to each outstanding share of common stock.

    Formula: EPS = (Net Income - Preferred Dividends) / Average Outstanding Shares

    Use: EPS helps assess a company's profitability on a per-share basis. Higher EPS indicates higher profitability. For example, if a company has a net income of ₹5,00,000, preferred dividends of ₹50,000, and 1,00,000 outstanding shares, the EPS would be ₹4.50 (₹4.50 per share).

  8. Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E Ratio)

    Definition: The P/E ratio compares a company's stock price to its earnings per share, indicating the market's valuation of the company.

    Formula: P/E Ratio = Market Price per Share / Earnings Per Share

    Use: The P/E ratio helps assess a company's valuation relative to its earnings. Higher P/E ratios generally imply higher market expectations. For example, if a company's stock is trading at ₹100 per share and has an EPS of ₹5, the P/E ratio would be 20x (20 times earnings).

  9. Dividend Yield

    Definition: Dividend Yield measures the return on investment from dividends distributed by a company.

    Formula: Dividend Yield = (Dividends per Share / Market Price per Share) x 100

    Use: Dividend Yield helps assess the income generated from dividends relative to the stock price. Higher dividend yield indicates higher returns from dividends. For example, if a company pays dividends of ₹10 per share and its stock is trading at ₹200 per share, the dividend yield would be 5%.
